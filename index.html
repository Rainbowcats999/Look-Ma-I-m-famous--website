Early years: Lincoln's early years were characterized by a harsh frontier upbringing in Kentucky and Indiana, marked by poverty, limited formal education, and a strong work ethic on his father's farm; he was born in a log cabin, experienced the tragic loss of his mother at a young age, and largely educated himself through voracious reading despite only attending school sporadically. Lincoln was born into poverty in a log cabin in Kentucky and was raised on the frontier, mainly in Indiana. He was self-educated and became a lawyer, Whig Party leader, Illinois state legislator, and U.S. representative from Illinois. In 1849, he returned to his successful law practice in Springfield, Illinois.

Birth and Family: Born on February 12, 1809, in a log cabin near Hodgenville, Kentucky to Thomas Lincoln and Nancy Hanks Lincoln.
Frontier Life: The Lincoln family moved to Indiana when Abraham was seven years old, settling in a pioneer community where he worked hard on the farm, clearing land and performing manual labor.
Limited Education: Due to the remote location, Lincoln received very little formal schooling, attending for only a few months at a time.
Loss of Mother: At the age of nine, Lincoln's mother died from "milk sickness," a disease transmitted by cows eating poisonous plants.
Stepmother's Influence: Following his mother's death, his father remarried Sarah Bush Johnston, who encouraged Lincoln's love of reading and learning.
Self-Education: Despite limited schooling, Lincoln became a keen reader, borrowing books and studying diligently in his free time, which significantly shaped his intellect.
Physical Strength: Lincoln was known for his strength and physical prowess, becoming a skilled wrestler in his youth.
